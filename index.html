Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The data visualization captures the cyclic nature of the data well. By plotting the data in a spiral, the visualization captures how the data changes over the course of the year.
  • The extreme contraction in new COVID cases in July 2021 gives me both the impression of how seasonal the virus is and how effective the vaccine rollout was.
  • By including the first few weeks of January in the data right at the end of the spiral the author demonstrates how rapidly the omicron was spreading at the time of the article.

Design Critique:

  • The design gives a good impression of the number of cases, but its lack of gridlines on the spiral makes it difficult to determine the exact number of cases at any given point. The small scale in the corner of the visualization is difficult to use due to its distance from the main visualization. This makes it difficult to make any quantitative ratio judgements about the data.
  • The original article was primarily about the new more virulent omicron varient of COVID yet the plot makes no distinction between the different varients of COVID. This makes it difficult to determine the impact of the omicron varient on the overall number of cases. This could lead the audience to conclude incorrect conclusions about the impact of the omicron varient on the overall number of cases or the effectiveness of public health measures.
  • In a similar vein one of the primary concerns at the time the article was written was hosptial capacity and the mortality rate of the omicron varient. By not including any information about hosptializations or deaths the visualization may not capture the success of the vaccine rollout in preventing severe cases of COVID or the reported lower hosptialization and mortality rate of the omicron varient.
  • The visualization also fails to capture transmission rates of the virus well. Because of the exponential nature of the virus, the number of cases can increase rapidly. By not including any information about the transmission rates of the virus the visualization does not capture the main idea of the article well that the omicron varient was spreading rapidly.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • Common baseline allows for direct quantitative ratio comparisons.
  • Direct comparison of data by month across different years. Very clear gridlines to make quantitative determinations easy to make.
  • Including 2022 projections into the future to reinforce the idea that the omicron varient was spreading rapidly and could have major impacts on the number of cases.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Capture the continuous nature of time more effectively with a line chart.
  • Still allow for easy quantitative comparison betweeen years.
  • Clearly demonstrate where the data turns into a projection and show the uncertainty in the projections.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • Facet out more of the components of the virus mentioned in the source article.
  • Make the comparisons more "fair" across years by making the data rates rather than absolute numbers.
  • Highlight that new cases does not directly translate to more hospitalizations or deaths due to immunity via the vaccine.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

For the final visualization I decided to go "back to the basics" as the visualization is geared towards a general audience so the most important element of the design is that the visualization is clear and easy to understand. Due to the time series nature of the data I chose the line mark with an x-axis representing time and a y-axis representing the number of new cases. To make quantitative ratio comparisons easier I chose to include gridlines that run horizontally across the data. I chose to also include the transmission rate through the visual encoding channels of both color and thickness in the line. To calculate the transmision rate I used the number of new cases in a day, divided by the number of new cases in the last 14 days (a proxy for active number of infected population). Transmission rate is a crucial element to understanding disease dynamics and by including this informaiton it helps the viewer understand how the public health measures introduced changed the spread of the disease even if new cases continued to tick up. It also helps compare across time as the transmission rate is a function of the number of current infected population helping to make more "fair" comparisons between years even if they had vastly different case numbers. I chose this dual encoding because it is difficult to make judgements for close points in terms of color but it is easy to make judgements about the thickness of the line. Similarly making judgements of thickeness for points that are far apart is difficult but making judgements about their color is easier. By using this dual encoding I hoped to add a new layer of information to the original visualization that highlights one of the important elements of the original article, the transmission rate. I chose to end the timeline roughly where the article ended to not give myself any unfair advantage in the prroducing a visualization as I am sure the original author would have made different decisions about the data if they had access to the data I do. An element of the data that I am worried is lost in my visualization is the impact of the vaccine and virus mutations on mortality and hospitalization rates. I chose not to include them as I wanted to keep the visualization simple, yet I worry that their omission may lead the viewer to incorrect conclusions about the impact of the vaccine and greater fears about the new omicron varient which could sew fear and panic in the general population.

Reflection

In creating the final visualization I was not able to address all of my critiques of the orignial visualization. I was able to make the data more quantitative and easier to understand but I was not able to address the lack of information about hospitalizations and deaths. In seeking to incorporate this informaiton I got a better understanding of the tradeoff between simplicity and density of information in a visualization. Whenever I tried to incorporate more information about the virus the visualization became more cluttered and difficult to understand. Ultimately I worried that this information overload would detract users from engaging with the visualization at all, or possibly lead to misunderstanding of the data presented. In keeping with my core critique of the original visualization I ensured that my visualization allowed for easy quantitative ratio comparisons had a common baseline and included gridlines. I also included some annotations ito the visualization to remind the reader of important dates and public health measures that were introduced. One phenomenon that the original visualization captured more effectively than mine was the cyclic nature of the virus. By using a spiral the original visualization lined up months across years and made the annual pattern more obvious. Overall, I believe I was successful in creating a visualization that was more easily understood and allowed for more quantitative ratio comparisons than the original.
